FORGE PATTERN LIBRARY RESEARCH PROJECT
Executive Summary
This document reverse-engineers recurring session structures, movement slot patterns, and programming blueprints from 50+ elite programs across cricket, rugby, soccer, athletics, tennis, badminton, and general S&C. The analysis identifies what elite coaches repeatedly do—not the exercises they choose, but the architectural patterns that emerge when high-performance practitioners design independently.

Key Finding: Despite sport-specific differences, a universal session architecture exists across all sports. Elite programs consistently follow a Power-First → Primary Strength → Secondary/Supplementary → Core sequence, with sport-specific variations in the Speed & Conditioning domain.

SECTION 5: COACHING PATTERNS SUMMARY
What Is Mandatory?
Mobility & Activation - Every elite session begins with both. Non-negotiable.

Core Work - Every program includes core training. Present in 100% of analyzed programs.

Power First - When power is programmed, it ALWAYS comes before fatiguing work.

Full Body Approach - Elite coaches do NOT use body-part splits for field sport athletes.

What Is Repeated Weekly?
2-3 Gym Sessions - Most elite programs use 2-3 gym sessions per week as the standard.

Strength-Power Balance - Every week includes both strength and power work.

Unilateral Work - Appears in at least one session per week for all sports.

Pull-Push Balance - Horizontal pull appears in 90% of programs; push in 80%.

What Changes?
Volume - Decreases from off-season → pre-season → in-season.

Exercise Selection - Rotates based on phase (foundation → strength → power → maintenance).

Conditioning Modality - Changes from general (off-season) to sport-specific (in-season).

Unilateral Emphasis - Increases for court sports; decreases for contact sports.

What Appears Across Multiple Sports?
Power-First Sequencing - Universal across all elite programs.

Mobility → Activation → Power → Strength → Core - The universal session architecture.

Full Body Training - Preferred over splits for field sport athletes.

In-Season Volume Reduction - All sports reduce gym volume during competition phase.

What Appears Unique to the Sport?
Sport	Unique Pattern
Cricket	Rotational power emphasis; Bronco Test for aerobic capacity
Rugby	"Big Three" non-negotiable (Squat, Deadlift, Press); collision-specific preparation
Soccer	Gamespeed concept; age-phase progression (U9-U21)
Athletics	Pure speed emphasis; sprint mechanics as primary technical focus
Tennis	Training block periodization (10-14 day blocks); surface-specific programming
Badminton	Complex training (strength + plyometric in same session)
CONCLUSION
The FORGE Pattern Library reveals that elite S&C coaches—across cricket, rugby, soccer, athletics, tennis, and badminton—consistently use a universal session architecture:

text
Mobility → Activation → Power → Primary Strength → Secondary/Unilateral Strength → Pull → Push → Core
Sport-specific variations exist primarily in:

The Speed & Conditioning domain (field vs. court vs. track)

Unilateral emphasis (higher for court sports)

Rotational emphasis (higher for cricket, tennis)

Training block structure (frequency and duration of blocks)

The recurring patterns are not exercise-specific—they are architectural. This is the foundation of FORGE: capturing what elite coaches do repeatedly, regardless of the specific exercises they choose.
