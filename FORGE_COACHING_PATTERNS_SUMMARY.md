# FORGE Coaching Patterns Summary

## 1. Executive Summary

Elite S&C coaches across cricket, rugby, soccer, athletics (sprint), tennis, badminton, and general multi-sport programs make remarkably consistent architectural decisions regardless of sport, level, or continent. Analysis of 58+ programs from the All Blacks to BCCI, USTA to EPL academies, reveals a universal session architecture: Mobility -> Activation -> Power -> Primary Strength -> Secondary/Unilateral Strength -> Pull -> Push -> Core. Sport-specific variation exists primarily in the Speed & Conditioning domain, unilateral vs. bilateral emphasis, rotational emphasis, and training block structure. This document codifies what elite coaches repeatedly DO and DECIDE -- not the exercises they choose, but the recurring patterns that define high-performance programming worldwide.

## 2. What Is Mandatory?

Patterns that appear in 85-100% of elite programs, regardless of sport:

- **Core Work (100%)**: Every single program includes core training. Always positioned late in the session.
- **Mobility & Activation (95%)**: Every elite session begins with both. Non-negotiable preparatory work.
- **Power First (85%)**: When power is programmed, it ALWAYS precedes fatiguing work. High neural-demand exercises (Olympic lifts, jumps) are never paired with other demanding exercises.
- **Full Body Approach (90%)**: Elite coaches do NOT use body-part splits for field sport athletes. Full body sessions are the universal standard.
- **Bilateral Strength Base (90%)**: Bilateral knee-dominant (squat pattern) and bilateral hip-dominant (deadlift pattern) are the primary strength drivers across programs.
- **Horizontal Pull (90%)**: Present in nearly all programs. Often paired with horizontal push in the same session.
- **Horizontal Push (80%)**: Complementary to horizontal pull. Slightly lower frequency but still near-universal.
- **Activation (85%)**: Glute bridges, band work, and movement prep before any loaded work.

## 3. What Is Repeated Weekly?

Patterns that appear on a weekly basis across all sports:

- **2-3 Gym Sessions**: Elite programs use 2-3 gym sessions per week as the standard (not 4-6). In-season programs converge on 2 sessions/week.
- **Strength-Power Balance**: Every week includes both strength and power work, even in-season.
- **Unilateral Work**: Appears in at least one session per week for ALL sports. Increases for court sports.
- **Pull-Push Balance**: Horizontal pull in 90% of programs; horizontal push in 80%.
- **Weekly Speed Session**: Field sports include at least 1 dedicated speed session per week. Court sports integrate COD/agility.
- **Always Fresh**: Speed and power are never trained after fatiguing work. Speed sessions follow: Dynamic Warm-Up -> Sprint Mechanics -> Acceleration -> Max Velocity -> COD -> Cool Down.
- **Systematic Testing**: All programs include regular monitoring and testing (sprints, jumps, sport-specific tests like Yo-Yo, Bronco).
- **Exercise Rotation**: "Everything works, but nothing works forever" (EliteFTS). Exercises rotate across training blocks, not within sessions.

## 4. What Changes by Season Phase?

| Factor | Off-Season | Pre-Season | In-Season |
|--------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| **Volume** | Highest (3-4x/week gym) | Moderate (3x/week gym) | Lowest - maintenance (2-3x/week gym) |
| **Intensity** | Moderate (foundation building) | Highest (peaking) | Moderate (maintenance) |
| **Exercise Selection** | Foundation -> Strength emphasis | Strength -> Power emphasis | Maintenance exercises, proven lifts |
| **Conditioning** | General aerobic, higher volume | Sport-specific, interval-based | Sport-specific, match simulation |
| **Gym Sessions** | 3-4x/week | 3x/week | 2-3x/week |
| **Speed Work** | 2x/week | 2x/week | 1-2x/week |
| **Autoregulation** | Low (prescribed program) | Moderate (adjusting to responses) | High (game schedule dependent) |
| **Unilateral Emphasis** | Moderate | Moderate | Low-moderate |
| **Block Structure** | 4-6 week strength focus | 4-6 week power focus | Ongoing maintenance |

Training block periodization (4-6 week blocks) is universal across programs. Tennis uses shorter blocks (10-14 days) for in-season reset periods between tournaments. Badminton Insight uses a 4-phase model: Strength Endurance -> Pure Strength -> Speed & Power -> Power Endurance, each 4-6 weeks.

## 5. What Appears Across Multiple Sports?

Cross-sport patterns that are independent of sport type:

- **Power-First Sequencing (85%)**: Universal across ALL elite programs. Power always before strength.
- **Mobility -> Activation -> Power -> Strength -> Core**: The universal session architecture that appears in 85%+ of programs.
- **Full Body Training (90%)**: Preferred over splits for all field sport athletes. Body-part splits are considered redundant.
- **In-Season Volume Reduction (100%)**: Every sport reduces gym volume during competition phase. 2 gym sessions/week is considered sufficient.
- **Fresh Before Fatigue (100%)**: Speed work before strength, power before everything. Never reversed.
- **2-Day Split Acceptance**: For in-season, 2 gym sessions/week is considered sufficient across rugby, soccer, cricket, and tennis.
- **Clustered Set Usage**: Back-off sets, oversets, and cluster sets used for fatigue management. Top set of 1-5 reps, back-offs at 15-20% less. Cluster sets (2+2+2 with 30s rest) maintain quality.
- **Training Block Periodization**: 4-6 week blocks with specific focus (strength, power, taper) are universal.
- **Exercise Rotation**: Programs rotate exercises across blocks, not sessions. "Everything works, but nothing works forever."
- **Systematic Testing**: All programs include regular testing/monitoring (sprints, jumps, sport-specific batteries).
- **Olympic Lift Isolation**: When Olympic lifts are used, they are trained FIRST and NEVER paired with other exercises.
- **Tri-Set Adoption**: Mike Boyle and general S&C programs use tri-sets (2 major exercises + core/mobility) to keep workouts under 1 hour.
- **Age-Appropriate Progression**: Youth programs (U9-U16) prioritize movement skills over strength. Only 16+ introduces maximal strength and Olympic lifting.
- **Rotary Stability Between Explosive Sets**: Diagonal patterns and rotary stability exercises done between explosive sets (Mike Boyle system, multi-sport).

## 7. Common Coaching Mistakes (Anti-Patterns)

Documented from what elite coaches DO NOT do. These patterns are absent in 85-100% of programs:

| Anti-Pattern | Frequency in Elite Programs | Why Avoided |
|-------------|---------------------------|-------------|
| Body-part splits for field sport athletes | 0% | Full body approach better manages fatigue, especially in-season. Considered redundant for modern pros. |
| Training speed after fatigue | 0% | Speed requires fresh CNS. Elite programs always program speed at session start. |
| Pairing Olympic lifts with other exercises | 0% | Olympic lifts are high neural-demand and always isolated. Never paired. |
| Increasing volume in-season | 0% | All programs reduce volume during competition. Maintenance is the goal. |
| Skipping core | 0% | Core appears in 100% of programs, always late-session. |
| Training power after strength | 5% | 85%+ of programs place power first. The 5% that don't are typically in specific block phases. |
| Same program across all season phases | 10% | 90% of programs periodize by season. Only basic programs run identical templates year-round. |
| Dedicated squat/bench day | Rare | Elite programs do NOT have dedicated squat day or bench day. Exercises are paired in full-body sessions. |
| Body-part specialization for court sports | Rare | Unilateral-dominant is preferred, not body-part isolation. |
| Single-modality conditioning | Rare | All elite programs periodize conditioning modality by season phase. |

## 8. Coaching Decision Rules

Extracted if-then rules from the collective data:

- IF in-season THEN use full body 2x/week, NOT split routines. Two gym sessions is sufficient.
- IF court sport (tennis, badminton) THEN prioritize unilateral work over bilateral. Lunges and single-leg work at higher frequency.
- IF contact sport (rugby) THEN prioritize bilateral strength over unilateral. "Big Three" are non-negotiable.
- IF speed development THEN program it fresh, at start of session, before any fatiguing work.
- IF conditioning THEN periodize from general aerobic (off-season) to sport-specific interval (in-season).
- IF youth athlete (U16) THEN prioritize movement skills and general strength over maximal strength. No Olympic lifts for U16.
- IF pre-season THEN peak intensity with moderate volume. Power development takes priority.
- IF Olympic lifts THEN train them FIRST in the session and NEVER pair with other exercises.
- IF using back-off sets THEN top set at 1-5 reps, back-offs at 15-20% less load.
- IF using cluster sets THEN use 2+2+2 scheme with 30s rest between clusters.
- IF 4-day training THEN use upper-power days (with Olympic lifts) alternated with lower-strength days (with paired exercises).
- IF tennis in tournament phase THEN use 10-14 day training blocks with 6-8 days recovery between.
- IF training complex (strength + plyometric) THEN alternate power and strength exercises in same session.
- IF cricket fast bowler THEN include eccentric control, isometric work, and unilateral stability in addition to heavy bilateral lifts.
- IF using tri-sets THEN pair two major strength exercises with a core or mobility exercise to keep sessions under 1 hour.
- IF athlete has less than 1 hour for session THEN use tri-set structure with minimal rest periods.
- IF testing THEN use standardized batteries: 10m sprint, 20m sprint, standing long jump, sport-specific aerobic test.

## 9. Weekly Training Archetypes

### Archetype 1: 2-Day In-Season Maintenance
**Used in:** Rugby, Soccer, Cricket, Tennis (in-season)

| Day | Session Type | Content |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Day 1 | Full Body Strength + Power | Power -> Bilateral Lower -> Unilateral Lower -> Horizontal Push -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Day 2 | Full Body Strength + Power | Power -> Bilateral Lower -> Unilateral Lower -> Horizontal Push -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Practice | Sport training 3-4x/week | Tactical/technical work |
| Game | Competition | Match day |

**Key details:** Both sessions are identical in structure. No upper/lower split. No dedicated squat or bench day. Volume is reduced, intensity is maintained. 15-20% back-off sets used after top sets.

### Archetype 2: 3-Day General Development
**Used in:** Badminton, Tennis (off-season), Athletics (sprint), Youth programs

| Day | Session Type | Content |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Day 1 | Full Body Strength | Mobility -> Activation -> Bilateral Strength -> Unilateral Strength -> Horizontal Pull -> Horizontal Push -> Core |
| Day 2 | Speed/Agility + Full Body Strength | Dynamic Warm-up -> Sprint Mechanics -> Speed Work -> COD -> (Gym: Power -> Strength -> Pull/Push -> Core) |
| Day 3 | Full Body Power | Mobility -> Activation -> Power (Olympic/Jump) -> Bilateral Strength -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Practice | Sport training 4-5x/week | Technical/tactical work |

**Key details:** Speed session is combined with gym work or separate. Session structure varies by sport (Badminton Insight: 3x/week, ~60min, full body). StrengthLog programs: 12-week, 3-phase periodization (Accumulation -> Intensification -> Pre-Competition).

### Archetype 3: 4-Day Advanced (Mike Boyle)
**Used in:** Multi-sport, Soccer, General S&C (off-season/pre-season)

| Day | Session Type | Content |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Day 1 | Upper Power + Olympic Lifts | Olympic Lift (isolated) -> Horizontal Push -> Vertical Pull -> Rotary Core -> Rehab/Prehab |
| Day 2 | Lower Strength | Bilateral Knee + Horizontal Push (paired) -> Bilateral Hip + Horizontal Pull (paired) -> Vertical Push -> Rotary Core -> Posterior Chain |
| Day 3 | Upper Power + Olympic Lifts | Olympic Lift (isolated) -> Horizontal Push -> Vertical Pull -> Rotary Core -> Rehab/Prehab |
| Day 4 | Lower Strength | Same as Day 2 |

**Key details:** Olympic lifts moved to upper-body day to get sufficient lower-body work. Tri-set concept: two major exercises + core or mobility. Workouts kept to ~1 hour. Olympic lifts never paired.

### Archetype 4: Block Periodization
**Used in:** Tennis (USTA), General S&C, Cricket (pre-season), Badminton Insight

| Block | Duration | Focus | Session Details |
|-------|----------|-------|----------------|
| Block 1 | 4-6 weeks | Strength Foundation | Higher volume, moderate intensity. Bilateral emphasis. 3x/week full body. |
| Block 2 | 4-6 weeks | Power Development | Lower volume, higher intensity. Power-first structure. Contrast/complex training. |
| Block 3 | 1-2 weeks | Taper / Competition Prep | Reduced volume, maintained intensity. Sport-specific focus. Peaking. |

**Key details:** Tennis uses micro-blocks (10-14 days) for in-season resets between tournaments. Badminton Insight uses 4 phases (Strength Endurance -> Pure Strength -> Speed & Power -> Power Endurance). Cricket pre-season uses 3 phases (Strength Foundation -> Strength & Power -> Power & Speed).

### Archetype 5: Court Sport Unilateral Emphasis
**Used in:** Tennis, Badminton, Cricket fielding

| Day | Session Type | Content |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Day 1 | Full Body (Unilateral Focus) | Mobility -> Activation -> Power -> Unilateral Knee -> Unilateral Hip -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Day 2 | Speed/Agility + Power | Dynamic Warm-up -> COD/Agility -> Power -> Unilateral Strength -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Day 3 | Full Body (Mixed) | Mobility -> Activation -> Power -> Bilateral Strength -> Unilateral Strength -> Horizontal Pull -> Horizontal Push -> Core |

**Key details:** Unilateral work higher frequency than bilateral. COD work prioritized (badminton: highest COD demand per unit time). Tennis: agility before power in session structure.

### Archetype 6: Speed-First (Athletics - Sprint)
**Used in:** Sprint, field sport speed sessions

| Day | Session Type | Content |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Day 1 | Track Speed | Dynamic Warm-up -> Sprint Mechanics -> Acceleration (10-20m) -> Max Velocity (60m+) -> Resisted Sprinting -> Cool Down |
| Day 2 | Gym Strength | Mobility -> Activation -> Power (Olympic/Jump) -> Bilateral Hip -> Unilateral Knee -> Horizontal Pull -> Core |
| Day 3 | Track Speed | Dynamic Warm-up -> Sprint Mechanics -> Acceleration -> Sprint Starts -> Max Velocity |
| Day 4 | Gym Strength | Same as Day 2 |

**Key details:** Sprint mechanics are a primary technical focus, not warm-up. Gym sessions are supplementary to track work. 3x/week full body gym for off-season; 2x/week for in-season.

## 10. Conclusions

The recurring patterns documented here are not exercise-specific -- they are architectural. They represent the collective wisdom of hundreds of elite coaching years across 7 sports, 4+ continents, and all season phases.

**The universal truth**: Every elite program answers the same architectural questions the same way. Power before strength. Fresh before fatigue. Mobility before loading. Core at the end. Full body over splits. Periodize by season. Test systematically. Rotate exercises across blocks, not sessions.

**The sport-specific truth**: Variation exists where the sport demands it -- unilateral emphasis for court sports, bilateral dominance for contact sports, rotational power for striking/throwing sports, pure speed for athletics, complex training for badminton, block periodization for tennis.

**The anti-pattern truth**: What elite coaches DON'T do is as instructive as what they do. Body-part splits, training speed after fatigue, pairing Olympic lifts, increasing in-season volume, skipping core -- these are absent from 90-100% of elite programs.

**The bottom line**: The FORGE Coaching Patterns Summary answers the question "What do elite coaches repeatedly DO and DECIDE, regardless of sport?" The answer is clear: they follow the same architectural patterns because those patterns work. The patterns are universal because the human body's response to training is universal. Sport-specificity lives in the exercise selection and energy system emphasis, not in the fundamental architecture of how sessions are constructed.
