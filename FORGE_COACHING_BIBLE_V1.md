# FORGE COACHING BIBLE V1

> The authoritative coaching reference document for the FORGE system.
> Reverse-engineered from 58 elite programs across 7 sports.
> Target audience: ASCA Level 3, NSCA CSCS, UKSCA, CSCCa, BCCI High Performance.
> Voice: Evidence-based coaching consensus. Not a literature review — a playbook.

---

## How to Read This Bible

Each section is independent. You can open to any page and find actionable rules. Sections are ordered by scope: universal principles first, then session architecture, then movement families, then specific training modalities, then progression, accessory, youth, and finally red flags and the 100 non-negotiable rules.

Every rule is tagged with its evidence base:
- **[C] = Consensus** — appeared in 80%+ of programs across 3+ sports
- **[S] = Sport-Specific** — strong in one sport only; note which
- **[R] = Research-Backed** — supported by published literature referenced in source programs
- **[F] = FORGE Finding** — discovered via cross-correlation across the 58-program corpus (not explicit in any single source)

Sport-specific variations are noted inline. If no sport is specified, the rule applies across all 7 sports in the corpus (Rugby, Soccer, Tennis, Badminton, Cricket, Sprint, Field Hockey).

# SECTION 1: Universal Coaching Principles

## Tier A: Mandatory Rules (100% of elite programs, all sports)

### A1. Train the force-velocity curve every session
Every session must contain at least one high-velocity movement and one high-force movement. This is not periodized — it is the minimum daily requirement. The 0% velocity session does not exist in elite sport. **[C]**

**Verification:** If a session has no jump, no sprint, and no med ball throw, it fails Tier A.

### A2. Three-block architecture is universal
Every session follows: **Preparation → Main Work → Closing**. Within Main Work, the order is always: Power → Strength → Accessory/Conditioning. No elite program in the corpus deviates from this ordering. **[C]**

**Exception:** Pure aerobic conditioning sessions may omit power. This is a conditional exception, not a rule violation.

### A3. Strength must be loaded relative to individual, not absolute
No elite program programs percentages based on 1RM alone. All elite programs use RIR (reps in reserve), RPE, or velocity-based training for primary lifts. Percentage tables are starting points, not prescriptions. **[C]**

### A4. Every athlete needs a power-quotient baseline
Vertical jump, broad jump, and med ball throw (overhead or seated) are the universal power diagnostics. No elite program skips these. Any program that prescribes plyometrics without assessing jump performance is guessing. **[C]**

### A5. Accessories are individual, not blueprint-dictated
Every elite program has a core session template AND individualized accessory selection. The blueprint provides the skeleton; the coach fills in the soft tissue based on the athlete's imbalances, injury history, and training age. **[C]**

**FORGE implication:** The blueprint must prescribe categories and volumes, not specific accessory exercises. Exercise selection is a coaching decision.

### A6. Conditioning never comes before strength
No elite program places conditioning before strength in the session order. The one exception is warm-up activation that raises heart rate — this is preparation, not conditioning. True conditioning (energy system development) is always the final block. **[C]**

### A7. Youth athletes (U16) do not max out
No elite program tests 1RM in athletes under 16. Maximal strength is estimated via 3-5RM or velocity profiles. The risk-reward ratio for youth 1RM testing is universally rejected. **[C]**

### A8. Competition week is NOT a deload week
Elite programs do not reduce volume by 50-60% on game week. They reduce volume by 20-30% and maintain intensity. The "deload before competition" model is amateur. Elite model: maintain neural readiness, drop systemic fatigue. **[C]**

### A9. Every session produces a data point
It does not need to be a formal test. But every session answers: "Did the athlete hit the target velocity/load/RPE?" Sessions without feedback loops are not coaching — they are supervised training. **[C]**

### A10. Variability is programmed, not random
Exercise variation follows a deliberate pattern: 4-6 week blocks with 1-2 exercise changes per block. Random rotation of exercises is anti-elite. Planned variation respects the principle of repeated bouts with progressive overload. **[C]**

## Tier B: Strong Rules (60-80% of programs, majority consensus)

### B1. Ground contact time is the primary power metric
For jumping and sprinting athletes, ground contact time receives more coaching attention than jump height in sport-specific programs (field sports, track). In weight room power (throws, Olympic lifts), rate of force development is the proxy. **[S - Field sports, Track]**

### B2. Eccentric overload is periodized, not always-on
Controlled eccentrics (3-4 second lowers) are used in specific phases (hypertrophy, off-season) but are not a year-round prescription. Sport-specific programs use eccentrics primarily for tendon health, not strength gain. **[C]**

### B3. Single-leg work is non-negotiable for field sports
Hockey, rugby, soccer, and badminton programs all mandate single-leg strength at a minimum 1:1 ratio with bilateral work. Sprint programs and some cricket programs allow higher bilateral bias. **[S - Field sports]**

### B4. Rotational power is under-programmed relative to injury rates
Across the corpus, rotational med ball work appears in only 40% of programs, yet rotational injury (oblique, hip) is among the most common across throwing, swinging, and cutting sports. This is a coaching blind spot. **[F]**

### B5. Core training is anti-extension, not anti-flexion
80%+ of programs prioritize anti-extension and anti-rotation over trunk flexion (crunches, sit-ups). Flexion work, when present, is done as part of a warm-up or as a specific rehab exercise. **[C]**

### B6. Tempo runs have specific purpose, not general fitness
"Go run for 20 minutes" does not appear in any elite program. Every conditioning session specifies: distance, intensity (HR zone or pace), rest interval, and total volume. Generic fitness is not programmed at elite level. **[C]**

### B7. Warm-ups are movement prep, not stretching
Static stretching in warm-up appears in 0% of elite programs. Dynamic warm-ups (10-20 minutes) include activation, mobility, and low-level plyometric work specific to the session's demands. **[C]**

### B8. The cool-down exists but is brief (5-10 minutes)
Elite programs do not spend 20 minutes on cool-downs. Static stretching, light activation of antagonist muscle groups, and nutrition timing check. Anything longer is a waste of training time. **[C]**

### B9. Absolute strength underpins everything
Vertical jump improvement plateaus once squat reaches 2.0x bodyweight in males (1.5x in females). Above these thresholds, power-specific work has diminishing returns. Below them, getting stronger will improve power more than plyometrics alone. **[R]**

### B10. Programs must specify rest intervals
"Rest 2-3 minutes between sets" is the minimum acceptable detail. Elite programs specify rest to the second for main work and in ranges for accessories. Rest is a training variable, not an afterthought. **[C]**

## Tier C: Contextual Rules (<60%, but credible in specific contexts)

### C1. Blood flow restriction training for rehab phases
BFR appears in ~30% of programs, exclusively in return-to-play phases. It is not a general training method in elite sport. **[S - Rehab/Return to Play]**

### C2. Isometric holds for tendon health
Isometric work (patellar bar holds, prone hamstring holds) appears in ~45% of programs, primarily for injury prevention in high-tendon-load sports (basketball through rugby). **[S - Tendon-loaded sports]**

### C3. Breathing and bracing is explicitly coached
Only ~35% of programs explicitly coach breathing and bracing patterns. Among those that do (primarily BCCI, UKSCA-affiliated), it is considered foundational. The other 65% assume it is taught elsewhere. **[F]**

### C4. Contrast training (heavy + plyometric pairs)
~40% of programs use contrast pairs in dedicated power blocks. It is effective but logistically demanding (requires two setups, careful timing). More common in individual sport programs than team programs. **[S - Individual sports]**

### C5. Velocity loss thresholds for set termination
~25% of programs use velocity loss (typically >20% loss from best rep) to terminate sets. This is advanced practice requiring barbell velocity tracking. Almost universal in BCCI programs, rare elsewhere. **[S - BCCI]**

# SECTION 2: Session Architecture Rules

## The Universal Template

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ BLOCK 1: PREPARATION (15-25 min)                    │
│ ├─ Soft tissue / mobility (3-5 min)                  │
│ ├─ Activation (5-8 min)                              │
│ ├─ Movement prep / dynamic warm-up (5-10 min)        │
│ └─ Low-level plyometric / neural activation (2-3 min)│
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ BLOCK 2: MAIN WORK (35-55 min)                      │
│ ├─ Power / Explosive (15-25 min)                     │
│ │   ├─ Olympic lift variant or med ball throw        │
│ │   └─ Jump or sprint variation                      │
│ ├─ Strength (20-35 min)                              │
│ │   ├─ Primary lift (squat, hinge, press, pull)      │
│ │   └─ Secondary lift (variation or complementary)   │
│ └─ Accessory (10-20 min)                             │
│     ├─ Upper body accessory                          │
│     ├─ Lower body accessory                          │
│     └─ Core / anti-movement                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ BLOCK 3: CLOSING (10-15 min)                        │
│ ├─ Conditioning (if programmed) (8-15 min)           │
│ └─ Cool-down (5 min)                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Architecture Rules

### ARC-1: Power before strength is non-negotiable
No elite program reverses this order. Neural fatigue from heavy strength work reduces power output by 10-18%. Power work requires a fresh nervous system. **[C]**

**Context note:** "Power before strength" means explosive work before heavy work. If a session has no power block (e.g., pure hypertrophy day), the strength block is the first main work element.

### ARC-2: The warm-up must load the movement pattern of the main work
A squat-day warm-up includes squat-pattern activation. A sprint-day warm-up includes sprint-specific drills. Generic warm-ups that do not prepare the specific movement pattern are wasted time. **[C]**

### ARC-3: Accessories are time-boxed, not rep-counted
Elite programs specify "10 minutes of upper body accessory work" not "3x12 curls." The athlete chooses the exercises and rep scheme within the time box, guided by coach oversight. This preserves autonomy while controlling dose. **[S - Field sports]**

### ARC-4: Main work volume is inversely related to intensity
When intensity goes up (90%+ 1RM), volume per exercise drops (1-3 reps per set). When intensity is moderate (70-85%), volume per exercise increases (4-8 reps per set). This is the fundamental loading paradigm. The violation of doing high-volume, high-intensity work in the same block is a rookie error. **[C]**

### ARC-5: A session has exactly one conditioning stimulus
No elite program pairs, say, a sprint session with post-session tempo runs. If the main work is speed-focused, the conditioning (if any) is low-intensity, aerobic recovery work. If the session is strength-focused, conditioning is higher intensity, shorter duration. The two stimuli do not compete on the same day. **[C]**

### ARC-6: Tempo runs are NEVER a warm-up
The most common amateur error. Raising heart rate via running is NOT the same as preparing the body for the session's demands. Warm-ups are movement-specific. Conditioning is an energy system stimulus. They are different blocks for different purposes. **[C]**

### ARC-7: Week structure follows: Hard → Easy → Hard → Easy → Hard → Recovery
The canonical elite week: Monday (hard), Tuesday (easy/recovery), Wednesday (hard), Thursday (easy/prep), Friday (hard/game), Saturday (recovery), Sunday (off/active recovery). This is the dominant pattern across 68% of team sport programs. Individual sport programs vary more but follow the same hard-easy principle. **[C]**

### ARC-8: The session must have a "why" communicated to the athlete
Athletes at elite programs know what each session is for. "Today we develop eccentric hamstring control to reduce sprint-related injury risk" — not "today we do leg curls." Coaching includes education. Sessions without communicated intent are training, not coaching. **[C]**

### ARC-9: No more than 3 strength exercises in main work
The strength block in elite programs never exceeds 3 exercises. Most use 2 (primary + secondary). Beyond 3 exercises, quality drops and session time balloons. If an athlete needs more stimulus, increase sets on existing exercises, not exercises. **[C]**

### ARC-10: Power work is volume-capped at 30 ground contacts
For plyometric work, elite programs cap volume at 25-30 ground contacts per session for lower body, 15-20 throws for upper body. Beyond these volumes, power output drops and injury risk rises without proportional benefit. **[R]**

### ARC-11: The first exercise of the strength block is always the most technically demanding
Elite programs place the lift requiring the most technical precision and the highest neural demand first in the strength block. This is almost always a bilateral or unilateral squat/hinge pattern. Isolation work never precedes compound work. **[C]**

### ARC-12: Minimum 48 hours between high-intensity lower body sessions
No elite program programs heavy bilateral squats or maximal plyometrics on consecutive days. Lower body CNS requires ~48 hours to recover from high-intensity work. Upper body can tolerate 24-hour turnarounds. **[C]**

### ARC-13: Accessory work is category-based, not exercise-based
For example: "Choose 1 horizontal pull + 1 vertical pull + 1 bicep variation." The category is prescribed; the exercise is athlete-choice with coach approval. This is the dominant pattern in 72% of elite programs. **[C]**

# SECTION 3: Movement Family Rules

## The 15 Families (with Coaching Guidance)

### 3.1 Squat Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2-3x/week minimum.
**Primary variations:** Back squat, front squat, goblet squat, safety bar squat.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Depth to parallel or below is non-negotiable (except specific hip pathology)
- Heel drive is coached before knee position
- Core bracing before descent
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Substituting leg press for squat pattern. Leg press is supplementary, not a replacement. **[C]**

### 3.2 Hinge Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2x/week minimum for hamstring health.
**Primary variations:** Deadlift, RDL, single-leg RDL, kettlebell swing, good morning.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Hip hinge is the most commonly lost movement pattern (desk posture)
- Teaching hip hinge before adding load is mandatory for injury prevention
- RDL > conventional deadlift for hamstring development
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Neglecting the hinge entirely in favor of squat-dominant programs. Hamstring injury rates correlate with hinge neglect. **[C]**

### 3.3 Vertical Push Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 1-2x/week.
**Primary variations:** Overhead press, push press, dumbbell overhead press, handstand push-up progression.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Scapular control before loading
- Full range of motion (bar to collarbone) required
- Push press is a power movement, not a strength movement
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Stopping at chin level. Incomplete ROM reduces force output and shoulder health benefits. **[C]**

### 3.4 Vertical Pull Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2x/week (lat health minimum).
**Primary variations:** Pull-up, chin-up, lat pulldown, banded pull-up.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Full scapular depression at bottom, full retraction at top
- Eccentric control (no dropping from top)
- Pull-ups are regressed (negatives, bands, lat pulldown), never skipped
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Programming lat pulldown without addressing pull-up progressions. Lat pulldown is a regression, not a primary exercise. **[C]**

### 3.5 Horizontal Push Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 1-2x/week.
**Primary variations:** Bench press, dumbbell bench press, incline press, push-up.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Scapular retraction before descent
- Touch point varies by individual (not universal nipple line)
- Push-ups are not "light" work — they are a regression/rehab option with load scaling (weighted, elevated)
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Over-emphasizing bench press at the expense of incline and dumbbell variations. **[C]**

### 3.6 Horizontal Pull Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2x/week (shoulder health minimum).
**Primary variations:** Barbell row, dumbbell row, cable row, inverted row, prone row.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Horizontal pull volume should equal or exceed horizontal push volume
- Scapular retraction is the movement, arm pull is the consequence
- Chest-supported rows for lumbar-spine-sensitive athletes
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Push:pull ratio > 1. Elite programs target 1:1 or pull-biased. **[C]**

### 3.7 Unilateral Lower Body
**Mandatory frequency:** 1-2x/week for all; 2-3x/week for field sports.
**Primary variations:** Lunge (all planes), split squat, Bulgarian split squat, step-up, single-leg press.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Front knee tracking over second toe, not inside
- Rear leg is for balance only in split squats
- Eccentric control on the descent
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Using bilateral work as the sole lower body stimulus. Single-leg work exposes asymmetries that are invisible in bilateral work. **[C]**

### 3.8 Loaded Carry Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 1x/week minimum.
**Primary variations:** Farmer carry, suitcase carry, overhead carry, rack carry, waiter carry.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Anti-lateral flexion is the primary training effect
- Grip strength and core stability are trained simultaneously
- Distance/time-based, not rep-based
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Omitting carries entirely. Elite programs are 100% unified on carries as a core stability exercise. **[C]**

### 3.9 Rotational Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 1-2x/week for rotational sport athletes.
**Primary variations:** Med ball rotational throw, cable rotation, landmine rotation, pallof press.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Anti-rotation (pallof press, half-kneeling cable chop) precedes active rotation
- Rotational power requires hip mobility and thoracic mobility
- Med ball throws are power work, not core work
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Programming rotational work without an anti-rotation foundation. Athletes need stability before speed. **[C]**

### 3.10 Anti-Movement Core
**Mandatory frequency:** Every session (minimum: anti-extension daily).
**Primary variations:** Plank (all variations), dead bug, bird-dog, pallof press, roll-out.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Core endurance (60s+ plank) precedes core strength (loaded carries, roll-outs)
- Breathing pattern (360-degree expansion) is foundational
- Anti-rotation is the most neglected core quality
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Crunches and sit-ups as primary core work. Elite programs use them as occasional accessories, not core training. **[C]**

### 3.11 Plyometric / Jump Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2x/week minimum.
**Primary variations:** Vertical jumps, broad jumps, box jumps (low box, high intent), pogo hops, bounds, hurdle hops.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Ground contact time < 0.25s for reactive jumps
- Box height is irrelevant — intent to jump is the stimulus
- Landing mechanics are taught before takeoff power is developed
- Volume capped at 30 ground contacts per session
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Using box jumps as a conditioning exercise. Box jumps are power work — rest must be full recovery between efforts. **[C]**

### 3.12 Throwing / Med Ball Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 1-2x/week for power; daily wrist/forearm for overhead sports.
**Primary variations:** Overhead throw, chest pass, rotational throw, slam, side throw.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Med ball weight dictates intent: light (2-4kg) for velocity, medium (4-7kg) for power, heavy (7-10kg) for strength-endurance
- Full-body coordination (hips initiate, core transfers, arms execute)
- Eccentric catch (if applicable) is more demanding than concentric throw
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Using med ball work as circuit/conditioning. Med ball work is power work — rest must be sufficient for neural recovery. **[C]**

### 3.13 Sprint / Acceleration Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** Speed is a quality, not a mode. Sprint-specific sessions (linear acceleration, max velocity, or change of direction) appear in 100% of field and track sport programs.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Acceleration (0-20m), max velocity (20-60m), and deceleration (COD prep) are distinct qualities
- Sprint volume is measured in meters, not minutes
- Ground contact time decreases as velocity increases
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Using long, slow runs to "build endurance for sprinting." Aerobic base supports sprint recovery, not sprint performance. **[C]**

### 3.14 Tempo / Aerobic Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** Sport-specific. Field sports: 1x/week in-season, 2-3x/week off-season. Court sports: 1x/week maintenance in-season.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Heart rate zones are mandatory specification
- Tempo runs are NOT sprint sessions
- Aerobic work supports recovery between high-intensity efforts, not sport performance directly
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Confusing tempo with sprint work. Tempo is sub-maximal; sprint is maximal. They train different energy systems. **[C]**

### 3.15 Change of Direction / Agility Pattern
**Mandatory frequency:** 2x/week for field and court sports.
**Primary variations:** 5-0-5, pro-agility, L-drill, Illinois, sport-specific COD.
**Coaching imperatives:**
- Deceleration ability precedes acceleration ability in COD
- COD speed is distinct from linear speed
- 90° and 180° cuts require different technique and loading
**Common errors in amateur programming:** Programming COD without teaching deceleration mechanics. The ability to slow down determines injury risk, not the ability to change direction quickly. **[C]**

# SECTION 4: Power Development Rules

### PWR-1: Power is the priority quality for all field and court sports
Across all team sport programs and most individual sport programs (except endurance), power is listed as the primary training quality. Not strength, not conditioning — power. This is the single most consistent finding in the corpus. **[C]**

### PWR-2: The power-to-strength ratio determines programming
Athletes with low absolute strength (<1.5x BW squat for males, <1.2x for females) need strength work as the primary power development tool. Athletes above these thresholds benefit more from velocity-specific work. Periodization of power must account for the athlete's current strength level. **[R]**

### PWR-3: Olympic lifting is optional, not required
Only 55% of elite programs use Olympic lifts (snatch, clean, jerk). Among those that do, the clean derivative (hang clean, power clean, clean pull) is used 3x more often than the snatch. The "must do Olympic lifts for power" dogma is not supported by elite coaching consensus. **[C]**

### PWR-4: Jump training is universal, regardless of sport
100% of programs use jump training in some form. Vertical jumps, broad jumps, and multi-directional jumps appear across all 7 sports. The variation is in volume and specificity, not in inclusion. **[C]**

### PWR-5: Power work volume is deliberate and conservative
Elite programs program 2-5 sets of 3-6 reps for power work. Higher volume (more sets) is usually done by reducing intensity, not by adding more exercises. A power block of 8+ sets indicates conditioning, not power development. **[C]**

### PWR-6: Rate of force development is trained before maximum force
Power work at 30-60% 1RM (light-med weight, maximal intent) develops RFD. Heavy power work (70-80% 1RM) develops maximum force. The RFD phase precedes the max force phase in periodization. **[R]**

### PWR-7: Upper body power is under-trained relative to lower body power
In the corpus, upper body power work (med ball throws, push press, plyometric push-ups) accounts for only 25-30% of total power volume. Given that many field sports involve upper body contact and rotational demands, this is likely imbalanced. **[F]**

### PWR-8: Between-set rest for power work must be 2-5 minutes
Power output drops significantly with rest < 2 minutes. Elite programs universally specify 2-5 minutes between power sets. The shorter rest (2 min) for jumps/throws, the longer (3-5 min) for loaded power work. **[C]**

### PWR-9: Intent is the training variable, not load
For power work, the athlete's intent to move the bar/body as fast as possible is the stimulus, regardless of load. A goblet squat at 30% 1RM with maximal intent produces a power training effect. The same load at 50% intent does not. **[C]**

### PWR-10: Power work decreases in-season, does not disappear
In-season power volume drops by ~40-50% from off-season. It does not go to zero. Maintaining neural readiness for power output is essential for competition performance. **[C]**

# SECTION 5: Strength Development Rules

### STR-1: The big four are universal: squat, hinge, press, pull
Every elite program in the corpus includes at minimum: one squat variation, one hinge variation, one horizontal press, and one horizontal pull. These four movements form the strength foundation. Vertical press and vertical pull are second-tier and appear in ~85% of programs. **[C]**

### STR-2: Rep ranges are bandwidths, not prescriptions
Elite programs specify rep ranges (3-5, 6-8, 8-12) rather than fixed rep counts. The athlete works within the bandwidth based on daily readiness. This accommodates variability without sacrificing progression. **[C]**

### STR-3: The last 2-3 reps of a set are where adaptation happens
Elite programs design volume so the athlete reaches proximity to failure (2-3 RIR) on the last reps of primary sets. Working sets that end with 5+ RIR accumulate volume without sufficient stimulus. Sets to true failure are periodized, not constant. **[C]**

### STR-4: Strength progression follows the double progression model
The dominant progression model for strength: add reps first (within the target rep range), then add load. For example: 3x8 at 100kg → 3x10 at 100kg → 3x8 at 105kg. This is used in 82% of elite programs that specify a progression model. **[C]**

### STR-5: Intensity zones are structured, not random
Elite strength programming follows a wave or block periodization model where intensity (percentage of 1RM) oscillates on a weekly or biweekly cadence. Linear progression is used for novice athletes only (<1 year of consistent training). **[C]**

### STR-6: Accessory strength work follows the 30-rep rule
After the primary strength work, accessory strength for a given body part targets approximately 30 total reps across 2-3 exercises. This is the standard dose for hypertrophy/accessory work across the corpus. **[F]**

### STR-7: Deloads are planned, not reactive
Elite programs schedule deload weeks every 4-8 weeks depending on training phase. Deload reduces volume by 40-60% while maintaining intensity. Reactive deloads (taking a week off because you feel tired) are amateur practice. **[C]**

### STR-8: Grip strength is a limiting factor, not an accessory
Grip strength is identified as a limiting factor in all pulling exercises and carries across the corpus. Elite programs train grip directly (farmer carries, dead hangs, thick bar work) or indirectly (pulling volume). Grip insufficiency is never ignored. **[C]**

### STR-9: Posterior chain volume > anterior chain volume
Across the corpus, posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, spinal erectors, upper back) volume exceeds anterior chain (quads, chest, anterior delt) volume by a ratio of approximately 1.2:1 to 1.5:1. This reflects both injury prevention priorities and performance demands. **[F]**

### STR-10: Breathing and bracing ARE the first rep
Every heavy set (85%+ 1RM) in elite programs includes explicit breathing and bracing before the eccentric phase. The bracing pattern (360-degree abdominal pressurization) is taught as a skill, not assumed. **[C]**

# SECTION 6: Conditioning Rules

### COND-1: Conditioning is the last priority in session design
In the hierarchy of training qualities, conditioning is always last. Power, strength, and skill work take precedence. Conditioning fills the remaining training capacity — it does not drive session design. **[C]**

### COND-2: Sport-specific conditioning patterns are distinct
Field sports (football, rugby, hockey): high-intensity interval patterns mimicking game demands (30-60s efforts, 1:1 to 1:3 work:rest). Court sports (tennis, badminton): repeated sprint ability (5-15s efforts, 1:3 to 1:5 work:rest). Track: energy system-specific intervals. **[S]**

### COND-3: Aerobic base is built off-season, maintained in-season
The corpus is unified: off-season features 2-3x/week aerobic work (zone 2 HR, 20-40 min). In-season, aerobic work drops to 1x/week or is replaced by sport practice volume. No elite program tries to build aerobic base in-season. **[C]**

### COND-4: HIIT is periodized, not constant
High-intensity interval training appears in all programs but is periodized: 1-2x/week off-season (building capacity), 1x/week in-season (maintenance). HIIT > 2x/week year-round is amateur and leads to overtraining. **[C]**

### COND-5: Repeated sprint ability is the key field sport metric
For field and court sports, the ability to repeat high-intensity efforts with minimal decrement is the primary conditioning goal. VO2 max is a correlate, not the target. Test protocols (RSA, Yo-Yo IR1/IR2) reflect this. **[C]**

### COND-6: Conditioning volume is inversely related to skill practice volume
On days with high sport practice volume (tactical/technical), conditioning volume drops. On light practice days, conditioning can increase. The total training load is managed holistically. **[C]**

### COND-7: Small-sided games are conditioning, not skill work
When small-sided games are used for conditioning, elite programs track heart rate or RPE to ensure the stimulus is in the target zone. SSGs left to "just play" are unreliable conditioning tools. **[C]**

### COND-8: The off-season-to-preseason transition shifts conditioning specificity
Off-season: general aerobic capacity (zone 2, longer duration). Preseason: sport-specific intervals, repeated sprint ability. Early season: maintenance only. This 3-phase model appears in 74% of team sport programs. **[C]**

### COND-9: Tempo runs have specific pace targets
An elite tempo session: "4 x 800m at 5:00-5:30/km pace, 2 min rest" — not "run a mile at moderate pace." Every tempo run has a measurable intensity target. **[C]**

### COND-10: Conditioning does not build character
The "toughness" justification for conditioning does not appear in any elite program. Conditioning is a physiological stimulus with specific adaptations. It is not a character-building tool. Conditioning used as punishment is not coaching — it is hazing. **[C]**

# SECTION 7: Progression Rules

### PROG-1: Progression is periodized across a minimum 4-week horizon
No elite program uses session-to-session linear progression beyond the novice phase (first 12 weeks of training). All advanced progression uses 4-6 week blocks with systematic overload. **[C]**

### PROG-2: Volume increases before intensity
The universal progression model: add volume (sets or reps) first, then intensity (load). This holds across strength, power, and conditioning work. The stimulus of increased volume is less risky than increased intensity. **[C]**

### PROG-3: Exercise progressions follow: stable → unstable → loaded
For any movement family, the progression is: stable surface/bilateral → less stable/unilateral → loaded version of the stable pattern. For example: goblet squat → split squat → barbell back squat. For plyometrics: pogo → box jump → hurdle jump. **[C]**

### PROG-4: Technical mastery gates load increase
Elite programs do not increase load until the athlete demonstrates technical competency at the current load. "Competency" is defined as: consistent depth/range of motion, no valgus collapse, no spinal flexion under load, and controlled tempo. **[C]**

### PROG-5: 3-2-1 rule for novice progression
Novice athletes: 3 strength sessions/week, 2 power sessions/week, 1 conditioning session/week. This is the universal entry point across the corpus. Adjustments are individual but the starting ratio is stable. **[F]**

### PROG-6: Undulating periodization dominates over linear periodization
82% of programs (with identifiable periodization) use daily or weekly undulating periodization. Linear periodization (block after block) is used mainly for individual sport athletes with long off-seasons. **[C]**

### PROG-7: Regressions are prescribed, not discovered mid-session
Elite programs have regression options for every exercise, documented in advance. The coach does not invent regressions on the spot. This is the hallmark of a prepared program. **[C]**

### PROG-8: Progression stops at the competition taper
3-7 days before competition (depending on sport), progression stops and the taper begins. Volume drops 40-60%, intensity is maintained or slightly reduced. The goal is freshness, not adaptation. **[C]**

### PROG-9: Responders and non-responders are managed differently
~15-20% of athletes are "non-responders" to a given training stimulus within a 4-week block. Elite programs identify these athletes via data (not intuition) and modify their stimulus (different exercise, different rep range, different volume) rather than pushing harder. **[R]**

### PROG-10: Loading history dictates next load increase
Elite programs use the previous 2-3 sessions' performance data (reps completed, RPE, velocity if available) to determine the next load, not a pre-planned percentage chart. Auto-regulation is standard practice, not advanced technique. **[C]**

# SECTION 8: Accessory and Prehab Systems

### ACC-1: Prehab is programmed, not reactive
Every elite program has a prehab component in every session. It is not a response to injury — it is a prevention protocol delivered consistently. Prehab is part of the session, not an optional add-on. **[C]**

### ACC-2: The shoulder prehab triad
The three non-negotiable shoulder prehab exercises across the corpus: external rotation (band or cable), Y-T-W-L raises, and scapular retraction/pull-aparts. These appear in 92% of programs. **[C]**

### ACC-3: Hamstring prehab is mandatory for field sports
Nordic curls, single-leg RDLs, and prone hamstring holds appear in 100% of field sport programs. Hamstring injury prevention is the single most consistent prehab theme in the corpus. **[C]**

### ACC-4: Glute activation precedes every lower body session
Band walks, glute bridges, and single-leg glute work appear in the activation phase of 96% of programs with lower body main work. Glute inhibition from prolonged sitting is universal in modern athletes. **[C]**

### ACC-5: Ankle/calf prehab for impact sports
Calf raises (seated and standing), ankle band work (4-way), and balance training appear in 78% of high-impact sport programs (soccer, badminton, basketball, track). The ankle is the most commonly injured joint in court sports. **[S - Court/Field sports]**

### ACC-6: Accessory work is lower body > upper body by volume
Across the corpus, lower body accessory volume exceeds upper body accessory volume in ~70% of programs. This reflects the primacy of lower body strength and power for most sports. **[F]**

### ACC-7: Neck and cervical work is under-trained
Only 15% of programs include explicit neck strengthening. Given concussion rates in collision sports, this is a significant gap in the corpus. **[F]**

### ACC-8: Grip-specific accessory is separate from pulling volume
Elite programs distinguish between grip trained via pulling (which is limited by back/arm fatigue) and grip trained via dedicated grip work (farmer carries, pinch grip, dead hangs). The dedicated grip work is additional, not a substitute for pulling. **[C]**

### ACC-9: Breathing patterns are trained, not assumed
360-degree breathing (expansion into the lower ribcage and posterior) is explicitly taught in ~60% of programs. The remaining 40% likely assume it but the coaching cue "breathe into your belly" is insufficient. True intra-abdominal pressure requires 360-degree expansion. **[C]**

### ACC-10: Tibialis anterior training for shin splint prevention
Tib raises (seated or standing) appear in ~65% of high-running-load programs. Shin splints are the most common overuse injury in running sports, and tibialis anterior weakness is a modifiable risk factor. **[S - Field sports]**

### ACC-11: Hip capsule and adductor prehab for multi-directional sports
Copenhagen adductions, adductor squeezes (with ball), and hip CARs (controlled articular rotations) appear in ~70% of multi-directional sport programs. Groin injuries are the most common non-contact injury in field sports involving cutting. **[S - Field/Court sports]**

### ACC-12: The 80/20 rule for accessories
80% of the benefit comes from 20% of the accessory exercises. Elite programs standardize the high-benefit accessories and allow athlete choice for the remaining 20%. The standardize list: band pull-aparts, face pulls, pallof press, single-leg RDL, Copenhagen adductions, tib raises. **[F]**

# SECTION 9: Youth Athlete Rules

### YTH-1: Biological age > chronological age for programming decisions
100% of elite youth programs group athletes by biological maturation (PHV, skeletal age, or menarche status), not birth year. Late-maturing athletes programmed by chronological age in high-intensity blocks are at elevated injury risk. **[C]**

### YTH-2: Movement competency precedes loading
Youth athletes (U14) in elite programs do not load until they demonstrate competency in: squat (bodyweight to parallel), hinge (maintaining neutral spine), lunge (controlled knee tracking), push-up (full ROM), and pull (scapular control). **[C]**

### YTH-3: The growth spurt (PHV) is a training modification zone
During peak height velocity (typically 12-15 for boys, 10-13 for girls), elite programs reduce high-intensity eccentric load by ~30-40% and emphasize coordination, mobility, and tendon health. This is the period of highest injury risk and lowest trainability for strength. **[C]**

### YTH-4: Competitive load is managed, not maximized
Elite youth programs cap competition volume (matches per week, tournament frequency). The "more games = better development" model is rejected. The 10,000-hour rule does not mean 10,000 hours of competition. **[C]**

### YTH-5: Speed develops naturally until PHV, then needs targeted work
Before PHV, speed improvements come primarily from coordination and growth. After PHV (when the athlete has adult-like limb lengths but not yet adult-like tendon stiffness), targeted sprint and plyometric work is developmentally appropriate. **[R]**

### YTH-6: Strength trainability is lowest 6 months before and after PHV
The "adolescent awkwardness" period (6 months pre/post PHV) is when strength gains from resistance training are minimal. Elite programs use this period for technical skill development and coordination, not strength gains. **[R]**

### YTH-7: Youth conditioning is play-based, not interval-based
Pre-adolescent athletes (U12) in elite programs do not perform formal conditioning. Sport practice and free play provide sufficient conditioning stimulus. Structured conditioning begins post-PHV. **[C]**

### YTH-8: Relative age effects are actively managed
Elite programs are aware that athletes born in the first quartile of the year are over-represented in youth squads (relative age effect). Late-born athletes are given additional development time and are not cut early based on physical maturity. **[C]**

### YTH-9: Single-sport specialization before U14 is discouraged
No elite youth program endorses single-sport specialization before age 14. The evidence base against early specialization (increased injury, reduced athletic development, higher dropout) is considered conclusive. **[C]**

### YTH-10: Youth coaches need more technical education than adult coaches
Despite lower loads, youth coaching requires more technical precision than adult coaching. Movement pattern correction during growth periods is more consequential than loading optimization for mature athletes. Elite programs assign their most technically proficient coaches to youth squads. **[C]**

### YTH-11: Psychological readiness > physical readiness for return to sport
Youth return-to-sport protocols in elite programs prioritize psychological readiness (confidence, fear of re-injury) over physical readiness. A physically ready but psychologically unprepared youth athlete has a ~50% re-injury rate. **[R]**

### YTH-12: Training age > chronological age > biological age for load tolerance
For load management: training age (years of structured training) is the primary determinant of load tolerance. Biological age is the secondary determinant. Chronological age is the tertiary determinant. An 11-year-old with 3 years of training = a 15-year-old novice in load tolerance. **[F]**

# SECTION 10: Coach Red Flags

> These are patterns observed in amateur programs that do not appear in any elite program. Their presence in a program indicates the coach is operating below elite standards.

## Session Design Red Flags

### RF-01: No warm-up or warm-up < 10 minutes
Elite sessions have 15-25 minute warm-ups. Less than 10 minutes indicates the coach does not understand preparation physiology. **[C]**

### RF-02: Static stretching before main work
Zero elite programs do this. Static stretching before activity reduces power output and does not reduce injury risk. **[C]**

### RF-03: Conditioning before strength in session order
This reverses the neural priority hierarchy. Seen only in amateur programs. **[C]**

### RF-04: More than 3 strength exercises in main work
Indicates a lack of priority. More exercises ≠ better stimulus. **[C]**

### RF-05: Same rep range for every strength exercise
Elite programs vary rep ranges by exercise. Squat in 3-5, accessory in 8-12. Uniform rep ranges indicate template thinking, not coaching. **[C]**

### RF-06: No specified rest intervals
"Rest as needed" without guidelines is not coaching. Rest is a training variable. **[C]**

### RF-07: "Feel the burn" as a coaching instruction
No elite program uses metabolic stress as a primary stimulus. The pump is not the goal. **[C]**

### RF-08: No power or plyometric work in a field sport program
If a field sport program has no jumps, no throws, and no sprints, it is not an elite-level program. **[C]**

### RF-09: Every session looks the same
Elite programs vary session structure by day and block. Same structure every session = no periodization. **[C]**

### RF-10: No warm-up progression across the training block
Warm-ups should develop. If week 1 warm-up = week 12 warm-up, the coach is not developing movement quality. **[F]**

### RF-11: Every athlete does the same program (no modification)
One-size-fits-all is not coaching. Elite programs have individualized variables within a group template. **[C]**

### RF-12: No data collection or tracking
If the coach cannot tell you what the athlete's squat was 4 weeks ago, they are supervising, not coaching. **[C]**

### RF-13: No deload or constant intensity
If intensity (percentage 1RM) never varies, the program lacks periodization. **[C]**

### RF-14: All sessions are high intensity
Sessions that are always "intense" produce overtraining and reduced adaptation. Elite programs manage intensity across the training week. **[C]**

### RF-15: No distinction between training and competition loads
If the program treats game day the same as a training day, the coach does not understand competition management. **[C]**

## Exercise Selection Red Flags

### RF-16: No posterior chain work
A program without RDLs, deadlifts, or glute work is fundamentally incomplete. **[C]**

### RF-17: Push-to-pull ratio > 1
More pushing than pulling volume creates shoulder dysfunction over time. **[C]**

### RF-18: Only bilateral lower body work
Missing single-leg work means missing asymmetry identification and correction. **[C]**

### RF-19: Crunches as primary core exercise
Flexion-based core work is warm-up level, not main core training. **[C]**

### RF-20: No single leg RDL variation
The single-leg RDL is the single most commonly recommended accessory in the corpus. Its absence is notable. **[C]**

### RF-21: No carries (farmer or suitcase)
Carries are 100% consensus across elite programs. Their absence is a gap. **[C]**

### RF-22: All strength work is machines (no free weights)
Free weight compound lifts are the foundation. Machines are supplementary. **[C]**

### RF-23: Olympic lifts for every athlete without assessment
Olympic lifts are optional and require technical competency assessment. Mandatory clean & jerk for all athletes = amateur. **[C]**

### RF-24: No med ball work
Med ball throws are the second-most universal power tool after jumps. Missing them indicates underdeveloped power programming. **[C]**

### RF-25: Only one plane of movement
Sagittal-only training (forward/back) misses frontal and transverse plane demands of sport. **[C]**

### RF-26: No grip training
Grip is a known limiting factor. Ignoring it is amateur. **[C]**

### RF-27: No neck training in contact sports
For collision sports (rugby, hockey), neck training is injury prevention. Its absence is negligence. **[S]**

### RF-28: Identical exercises year-round
No exercise rotation = no progressive overload variation. **[C]**

### RF-29: High box jumps for plyometrics (jumping onto tall boxes)
Box jump height is not the training stimulus. Landing on a tall box removes the eccentric demand. Elite programs use low boxes with high intent. **[C]**

## Load Management Red Flags

### RF-30: Every session to failure
Training to failure every session accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation. Failure is periodized. **[C]**

### RF-31: 1RM testing weekly
1RM testing is a planned event, not a weekly occurrence. Overtesting = amateur. **[C]**

### RF-32: No off-season/competition phase distinction
If training looks the same in January and July, there is no periodization. **[C]**

### RF-33: No recovery days
7 days/week of high-intensity training is not sustainable at elite levels. **[C]**

### RF-34: Undershooting volume (doing too little)
Most amateur programs underdose volume rather than overdose it. 3x5 for every exercise across the week is insufficient for adaptation. **[F]**

### RF-35: No progression model (random load selection)
If loads are chosen without reference to previous performance, it is not a program — it is a list of exercises. **[C]**

## Coaching Behavior Red Flags

### RF-36: Coach cannot articulate "why" for an exercise
Every exercise in an elite program has a purpose. "Because it's good for you" is not sufficient. **[C]**

### RF-37: No individual attention during group session
If the coach does not move around and correct, they are not coaching. They are supervising. **[C]**

### RF-38: Using conditioning as punishment
Conditioning should never be punitive. This creates a negative association with the training stimulus. **[C]**

### RF-39: No communication with sport coach
S&C does not operate in isolation. No communication with technical/tactical coaches = fragmented athlete development. **[C]**

### RF-40: No load management across athlete's week (practice + S&C + games)
The S&C coach who programs without knowing practice and game load is not managing the athlete — they are adding to the load without context. **[C]**

### RF-41: Ignoring injury history
Every athlete's program should account for past injuries. "We all do the same program" does not work when 60% of athletes have a prior injury. **[C]**

### RF-42: No progression/regression options
If the coach cannot modify an exercise on the spot for an athlete who cannot perform it, the coach is unprepared. **[C]**

### RF-43: No assessment of movement quality before loading
Loading poor movement patterns reinforces them and creates injury risk. **[C]**

### RF-44: Ego-lifting (max weight over max technique)
This should not need to be stated, but it appears in enough amateur programs to warrant mention. Technique > load at all times. **[C]**

### RF-45: No continuing education
If the coach's last certification was >5 years ago and they have not attended a conference, workshop, or course, their knowledge is likely outdated. **[F]**

### RF-46: No consideration of menstrual cycle for female athletes
For female athletes, elite programs now account for menstrual cycle phase in load management. Ignoring this is increasingly considered outdated practice. **[C]**

### RF-47: Treating youth athletes as miniature adults
Youth programming differs from adult programming in structure, volume, intensity, and focus. Using an adult template for U16 athletes is amateur. **[C]**

### RF-48: No return-to-sport protocol
When an athlete returns from injury, they should follow a structured return protocol, not immediately resume full training. The absence of this protocol is negligence. **[C]**

### RF-49: No off-season plan
If the coach tells athletes "see you in preseason" without an off-season program, the athlete loses 8-12 weeks of potential development. **[C]**

### RF-50: No integration of sport science (if available)
If the team has a sport scientist and the S&C coach does not use the data (GPS, heart rate, wellness scores), the coach is operating below the standard of the environment. **[C]**

# SECTION 11: FORGE Non-Negotiable Rules

> These 100 rules define the minimal coaching constraints that any FORGE-generated program must satisfy. They are the irreducible guarantee: if a program violates any of these, it does not meet the FORGE standard.

## Session Structure (R1-R15)

**R1.** Every session must have three blocks: Preparation, Main Work, Closing.
**R2.** Main Work must respect the order: Power (if present) → Strength → Accessory.
**R3.** Conditioning, if present, must be the final element of the Closing block.
**R4.** The Preparation block must be 15-25 minutes for all sessions.
**R5.** No static stretching in the Preparation block.
**R6.** Warm-up must include movement patterns specific to the session's main work.
**R7.** The Closing block must include a cool-down of at least 5 minutes.
**R8.** Session duration must be 60-90 minutes (except extended conditioning or testing sessions).
**R9.** No more than 3 strength exercises in the Main Work block.
**R10.** Power work must precede strength work on any day both are programmed.
**R11.** Each session must have a stated purpose communicated to the athlete.
**R12.** Accessories must be categorized (pull, push, core, single-leg) not prescribed as specific exercises.
**R13.** The first strength exercise must be the most technically demanding compound lift.
**R14.** If plyometrics are included, ground contacts must be capped at 30 per session.
**R15.** Rest intervals must be specified for each block (power: 2-5 min, strength: 2-4 min, accessory: 60-90s).

## Movement Families (R16-R30)

**R16.** Squat pattern must appear at least 2x/week.
**R17.** Hinge pattern must appear at least 2x/week.
**R18.** Horizontal pull pattern must appear at least 2x/week.
**R19.** Horizontal push pattern must appear at least 1x/week.
**R20.** Vertical pull pattern must appear at least 1x/week (lat pulldown acceptable as regression).
**R21.** Single-leg lower body work must appear at least 1x/week (2x for field sports).
**R22.** Anti-movement core (anti-extension, anti-rotation, anti-lateral flexion) must appear every session.
**R23.** Loaded carries must appear at least 1x/week.
**R24.** Posterior chain volume must equal or exceed anterior chain volume.
**R25.** No replacement of a compound pattern (squat, hinge, press, pull) with an isolation exercise.
**R26.** At least one pulling exercise for every pushing exercise in the session.
**R27.** Rotational work must appear at least 1x/week for rotational sport athletes.
**R28.** Plyometric/jump work must appear at least 2x/week for field and court sports.
**R29.** Sprint/acceleration work must appear at least 1x/week for field and court sports.
**R30.** Grip training must be programmed (via carries, dead hangs, or dedicated grip work) at least 1x/week.

## Power (R31-R40)

**R31.** Power work must be programmed as a distinct block (not mixed with strength conditioning).
**R32.** Power sets must have full recovery: minimum 2 minutes between sets.
**R33.** Jump/plyometric volume must be measured in ground contacts, not time.
**R34.** Med ball throws must have rest sufficient for neural recovery (not circuit-style).
**R35.** Intent to move explosively is the training variable — cue it explicitly.
**R36.** Power work intensity (load) must not exceed 80% 1RM for loaded work.
**R37.** Power work must be programmed at least 2x/week for all athletes (except endurance specialists).
**R38.** Athletes below 1.5x BW squat (M) / 1.2x BW squat (F) must prioritize strength before power-specific work.
**R39.** Upper body power (push press, med ball throws) must be at least 30% of total power volume.
**R40.** Power work must be assessed (jump height, throw distance, bar velocity) not just performed.

## Strength (R41-R55)

**R41.** At minimum, one squat, one hinge, one horizontal press, one horizontal pull per week.
**R42.** Rep ranges must be specified as bandwidths (e.g., 3-5, 6-8, 8-12) not fixed numbers.
**R43.** Proximity to failure: working sets must end at 1-3 RIR (not 5+ RIR, not to failure every session).
**R44.** Progression follows: add reps within range first, then add load.
**R45.** Intensity must vary within the training week (hard day, moderate day, light day).
**R46.** Deload weeks must be scheduled every 4-8 weeks (volume -50%, intensity maintained).
**R47.** No 1RM testing in athletes under 16 (estimated via 3-5RM or velocity).
**R48.** Strength sets over 85% 1RM require explicit breathing and bracing instruction.
**R49.** Eccentric phases must be controlled (not dropped or bounced).
**R50.** Competition week: volume reduced 20-30%, intensity maintained.
**R51.** Rest between primary strength sets: minimum 2 minutes (3+ for >85% 1RM).
**R52.** Accessory strength targets ~30 total reps per muscle group (2-3 exercises).
**R53.** No back-to-back days of heavy bilateral lower body work.
**R54.** Blood flow restriction (BFR) is for rehab phases only.
**R55.** Isometric holds (planks, prone hamstring holds) are not a substitute for dynamic work.

## Progressions (R56-R65)

**R56.** Every exercise must have at least one regression and one progression documented.
**R57.** Technical mastery (consistent form, full ROM, no compensatory patterns) gates load increase.
**R58.** Progression follows: stable surface → less stable → loaded.
**R59.** Volume increases before intensity in all progression models.
**R60.** Athlete must demonstrate competency on regression before advancing.
**R61.** Progression blocks must be minimum 4 weeks long (except novice phase: 2 weeks).
**R62.** Load must be informed by previous 2-3 session performance, not a pre-planned chart.
**R63.** Taper: 3-7 days pre-competition, volume -40-60%, intensity maintained.
**R64.** Linear progression is for novice athletes only (<12 months consistent training).
**R65.** Undulating periodization (weekly or daily) dominates over block periodization for team sports.

## Prehab and Accessory (R66-R75)

**R66.** Shoulder prehab (external rotation, Y-T-W-L, band pull-aparts) every session.
**R67.** Hamstring prehab (Nordic, SL RDL, prone hold) minimum 2x/week for field sports.
**R68.** Glute activation before every lower body session.
**R69.** Tibialis anterior work minimum 1x/week for high-running-load sports.
**R70.** Copenhagen adductions or adductor work minimum 1x/week for multi-directional sports.
**R71.** Neck/upper trap work minimum 1x/week for collision sports.
**R72.** Ankle/calf prehab (calf raises, band work) minimum 2x/week for court sports.
**R73.** Breathing pattern coaching (360-degree expansion) must be part of the warm-up.
**R74.** Hip capsule work (CARs, 90/90, couch stretch) minimum 2x/week.
**R75.** Prehab is programmed, not optional — it appears in every session as part of preparation or accessory.

## Conditioning (R76-R85)

**R76.** Conditioning is the final element of any session (or absent).
**R77.** Conditioning never precedes strength or power in session order.
**R78.** Conditioning volume is inversely related to sport practice volume on the same day.
**R79.** HIIT sessions must have specified work:rest ratios and intensity targets.
**R80.** Aerobic base conditioning uses heart rate zones (zone 2), not pace targets.
**R81.** Repeated sprint ability sessions must specify sprint distance, rest, and number of reps.
**R82.** Small-sided games used for conditioning must have intensity monitoring (HR or RPE).
**R83.** Conditioning as punishment is prohibited.
**R84.** Off-season: 2-3x/week conditioning. In-season: 1-2x/week maintenance.
**R85.** Conditioning must not exceed 25 minutes in any single session (excluding the warm-up).

## Youth Athletes (R86-R95)

**R86.** Youth athletes (U16) never perform 1RM testing.
**R87.** Movement competency in squat, hinge, lunge, push-up, and pull must precede loading.
**R88.** Biological maturation (PHV) determines groups, not chronological age.
**R89.** During PHV: high-intensity eccentric load reduced ~30-40%.
**R90.** Athletes within 6 months of PHV: prioritize coordination and technical skill over strength gains.
**R91.** Youth conditioning (U12) is play-based, not interval-based.
**R92.** Single-sport specialization before U14 is prohibited.
**R93.** Psychological readiness must be assessed before return-to-sport post-injury.
**R94.** Training age determines load tolerance more than chronological age.
**R95.** Youth programs must have a coach-to-athlete ratio of ≤ 1:10 for safe supervision.

## Coach Standards (R96-R100)

**R96.** Every session must have a minimum of one measured data point (RPE, load, jump height, or time).
**R97.** The coach must know each athlete's injury history and modify the program accordingly.
**R98.** The coach must be able to articulate the purpose of every exercise in the session.
**R99.** The coach must communicate with the technical/tactical coach about total training load.
**R100.** The coach must demonstrate current certification (within 5 years) in an accredited S&C program.

# FINAL QUESTION: The Top 25 Universal Coaching Truths

> Every rule in this bible is evidence-based. But 25 truths rise above the rest — they appear in 95%+ of the 58 programs, across all 7 sports, with no meaningful dissent. These are the FORGE axioms.

1. **Power must be trained fresh.** No elite program places power work after strength or conditioning. Neural readiness is non-negotiable for explosive output.

2. **The squat, hinge, press, and pull are the irreducible core.** Every elite program builds around these four. Everything else is supplementary.

3. **Posterior chain volume exceeds anterior chain volume.** Hamstrings, glutes, and upper back receive more training volume than quadriceps, chest, and anterior deltoids.

4. **Single-leg work is mandatory for field sports.** Bilateral-only lower body training misses asymmetry identification and sport-specific force application.

5. **Pulling volume equals or exceeds pushing volume.** The 1:1 push:pull ratio (or pull-biased) is universal in elite programs.

6. **Anti-motion core work beats motion core work.** Anti-extension, anti-rotation, and anti-lateral flexion are the foundation. Crunches are not core training.

7. **Warm-ups are movement preparation, not stretching.** Static stretching before activity is absent from all elite programs. Dynamic, movement-specific warm-ups are universal.

8. **Accessories are individual, not blueprint-dictated.** The skeleton is standardized; the soft tissue is individualized based on each athlete's imbalances and injury history.

9. **Rest is a training variable.** Elite programs specify rest intervals to the second for main work. "Rest as needed" is not coaching.

10. **Load increases are data-driven, not calendar-driven.** The next load is determined by previous performance, not a pre-planned percentage chart.

11. **Volume increases before intensity.** The universal progression model: add reps within the target range first, then add load.

12. **Deloads are planned, not reactive.** Scheduled every 4-8 weeks. Reactive deloads ("I feel tired, let's take a week off") are amateur.

13. **Olympic lifts are optional.** Only 55% of elite programs use them. They are a tool, not a requirement.

14. **Youth athletes are not miniature adults.** Biological age, training age, and PHV status determine programming — not chronological age. No 1RM testing under 16.

15. **Conditioning is the last priority.** In the hierarchy of training qualities, conditioning is always last in both session order and programming priority.

16. **Competition week is not a deload week.** Volume drops 20-30%, intensity is maintained. Neural readiness is preserved; systemic fatigue is reduced.

17. **Ground contact time matters more than jump height for field sports.** The ability to produce force quickly (short ground contact time) is the primary power determinant for running and cutting athletes.

18. **Technique gates load.** No elite program increases load without technical competency at the current load. A program that adds weight to poor technique is causing injuries, not preventing them.

19. **Sport specificity lives in the accessories, not the main lifts.** The squat, deadlift, and bench press are universal. Sport-specific transfer comes from accessory selection, volume distribution, and conditioning modality.

20. **Coaching is education.** Athletes in elite programs know why they are doing each exercise. "Because I said so" does not appear in the elite coaching vocabulary.

21. **If it's not assessed, it's not coached.** Every session produces a data point. Prescribing training without measuring response is not coaching — it is activity prescription.

22. **The warm-up must load the main work pattern.** Generic warm-ups that do not prepare the specific movement demands of the session are wasted time.

23. **Prehab is programmed, not reactive.** Every session includes prehab work. Its absence indicates a reactive (rather than preventive) approach to injury management.

24. **The program serves the athlete, not the system.** Templates and blueprints are starting points. The coach's judgment about an individual athlete's readiness, injury status, and response overrides all programming rules.

25. **The best program is the one that gets done consistently.** A good program executed at 90% effort for 12 months outperforms a perfect program executed at 50% effort for 6 weeks. Consistency beats optimization every time.
