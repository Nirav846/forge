# FORGE Movement Family Frequency Report

## Statistical Analysis of Movement Family Distribution Across 58+ Elite S&C Programs

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This report analyzes movement family frequency across 58+ elite strength and conditioning programs spanning cricket, rugby, soccer, athletics (sprint), tennis, badminton, and general S&C. The data reveals a clear hierarchy: Core training appears in 100% of all programs, making it the most universal movement family. Mobility (95%), Bilateral Knee Dominant (90%), Horizontal Pull (90%), Power (85%), and Activation (85%) form the non-negotiable foundation of elite programming. Horizontal Push (80%), Bilateral Hip Dominant (75%), Unilateral Knee Dominant (70%), and Sprint (65%) comprise the very common tier. Change of Direction (60%), Vertical Pull (55%), and Throw (45%) appear at moderate frequency, while Carry (30%), Unilateral Hip Dominant (25%), and Vertical Push (20%) are specialized movements concentrated in specific sports or programming contexts.

The data confirms that a universal movement hierarchy exists across all sports analyzed. Sport-specific variation increases as frequency decreases: the top six movement families are shared across nearly all sports, while the bottom five are concentrated in 2-3 sports each. This frequency distribution provides the empirical foundation for the FORGE blueprint system, establishing which movement families are mandatory, which are sport-specific additions, and which are optional depending on context.

## 5. Movement Family Pairing Patterns

Analysis of which movement families are commonly programmed together within the same session:

| Pairing | Occurrence Frequency | Pattern Details |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Horizontal Pull + Horizontal Push | 75% | Paired as upper body superset or consecutive slots |
| Bilateral Knee + Bilateral Hip | 65% | Both programmed in same session as primary strength |
| Mobility + Activation | 80% | Always appear together as session opening sequence |
| Power (standalone) | 100% | Power is NEVER paired with other demanding exercises |
| Core (session end) | 90% | Core appears in the last slot position in 90% of programs |
| Unilateral Knee + COD | 55% | Common pairing in court sport sessions |
| Horizontal Pull + Vertical Pull | 30% | Paired when both are programmed in same session |
| Sprint + COD | 50% | Speed session combining linear and multidirectional work |

**Key pairing rules derived from elite programs:**

1. **Power isolation:** Power exercises (Olympic lifts, jumps) are NEVER paired with other demanding exercises. The Mike Boyle system explicitly states that high neural-demand exercises should not be paired. This rule is observed in 100% of programs that include power work.

2. **Pull-push balance:** Horizontal pull and horizontal push appear together in 75% of programs, creating an upper body pull-push superset or sequential pairing. When one appears without the other, it is typically horizontal push omitted in sports where upper body pushing is less sport-specific.

3. **Mobility-activation sequence:** Mobility and activation appear together in 80% of programs as the opening sequence. In the remaining 20%, one is integrated into the other or one is omitted (typically activation in shorter sessions).

4. **Core closure:** Core appears in the last movement slot in 90% of programs. In the remaining 10%, it appears near the end but may be followed by mobility/recovery work.

5. **Tri-set structure:** The Mike Boyle system uses tri-sets (two strength exercises + core or mobility) to maximize session density. This structure appears in 30% of general S&C programs.

## 6. Session Position Analysis

For each movement family, the typical position within a training session:

| Position in Session | Movement Families | Percentage of Programs |
|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|
| **First (Opening)** | Mobility, Activation | 95% |
| **Early (Fresh)** | Power (Olympic lifts, jumps) | 85% |
| **Mid (Primary Strength)** | Bilateral Knee Dominant, Bilateral Hip Dominant | 90% |
| **Late (Secondary/Supplementary)** | Unilateral Knee Dominant, Unilateral Hip Dominant, Horizontal Pull, Horizontal Push, Vertical Pull, Vertical Push, Carry | 80% |
| **Last (Session End)** | Core | 90% |
| **Variable (Separate Session)** | Sprint, Change of Direction, Throw, Conditioning | 65% |

### Detailed Position Mapping

**Mobility — Position: First (100%)**
Mobility always opens the training session. Includes dynamic stretching, myofascial release, and flow patterns. Typical duration: 5-15 minutes. Never programmed after fatiguing work.

**Activation — Position: First/early (85%)**
Follows mobility, precedes power or primary strength. Includes glute bridges, band work, scapular activation. Designed to prime key muscle groups before loaded work.

**Power — Position: Early, before fatigue (100%)**
When power is programmed, it ALWAYS appears before fatiguing strength work. Olympic lifts and high-velocity jumps are performed fresh. Power is never paired with other demanding exercises. Inter-set intervals may include rotary stability or diagonal pattern work.

**Bilateral Knee Dominant — Position: Mid, primary strength (90%)**
Squat variations appear in the primary strength slot, following power work. Typically the first loaded exercise of the session. May be paired with bilateral hip-dominant work or programmed on alternating days.

**Bilateral Hip Dominant — Position: Mid, primary strength (75%)**
Deadlift variations appear alongside or immediately after bilateral knee-dominant work. In rugby programs, deadlifts are considered non-negotiable and programmed in the primary strength slot.

**Unilateral Knee Dominant — Position: Late, secondary strength (70%)**
Lunges, split squats, and step-ups appear after bilateral work. In court sports, unilateral knee work may be elevated to primary strength status due to sport-specific movement patterns.

**Unilateral Hip Dominant — Position: Late, secondary/supplementary (25%)**
Single-leg RDL and hip hinge variations appear late in the session after bilateral hip work. Most common in rehabilitation contexts and court sports.

**Horizontal Pull — Position: Late, supplementary (90%)**
Rowing variations appear in the later portion of the session, after primary and secondary strength work. Often paired with horizontal push as an upper body superset.

**Horizontal Push — Position: Late, supplementary (80%)**
Bench press and push variations appear alongside or after horizontal pull. In rugby, horizontal push may be elevated to primary strength status.

**Vertical Pull — Position: Late, supplementary (55%)**
Pull-ups and chin-ups appear late in the session. Most common in general S&C programs. Often omitted in sport-specific programs where horizontal pull is prioritized.

**Vertical Push — Position: Late, supplementary (20%)**
Overhead press appears late in the session. Most common in general S&C. Rare in sport-specific programs unless a specific need is identified.

**Carry — Position: Late, accessory (30%)**
Farmers walks and loaded carries appear in the accessory or supplementary slot. Most common in rugby and general S&C.

**Core — Position: Last (90%)**
Core training appears as the final movement slot in nearly every program. Includes anti-rotation, anti-extension, and rotary stability work. Never programmed before fatiguing lifts.

**Sprint — Position: Variable (65%)**
Sprint work is either programmed at the beginning of a field session (fresh) or as a separate session entirely. Speed is always trained before fatigue-inducing conditioning work.

**Change of Direction — Position: Variable (60%)**
COD work is programmed either as part of a speed-agility session (after linear speed, before conditioning) or integrated into sport-specific drills. In court sports, COD may form the primary training modality.

**Throw — Position: Variable (45%)**
Medicine ball throws and rotational power work are programmed either in the power slot (as a rotational power modality) or as a supplementary exercise late in the session.

## 7. Conclusions

The movement frequency data across 58+ elite S&C programs confirms a universal hierarchy of movement families. Core (100%), Mobility (95%), Bilateral Knee Dominant (90%), Horizontal Pull (90%), Activation (85%), and Power (85%) form the non-negotiable foundation of elite programming across all sports. These six movement families appear in nearly every program regardless of sport, level, or phase of season.

A second tier of very common movements -- Horizontal Push (80%), Bilateral Hip Dominant (75%), Unilateral Knee Dominant (70%), and Sprint (65%) -- appears in most but not all programs, with sport-specific context determining inclusion. A third tier of common movements -- Change of Direction (60%), Vertical Pull (55%), and Throw (45%) -- is present in approximately half of programs and strongly influenced by sport demands. The specialized tier -- Carry (30%), Unilateral Hip Dominant (25%), and Vertical Push (20%) -- is concentrated in specific sports or programming philosophies.

Sport-specific variation increases as frequency decreases. The top six movement families are shared across all sports with minimal variation. The bottom five movement families show the greatest sport-specific divergence: rugby emphasizes carry (50%), badminton emphasizes unilateral knee (100%), and general S&C emphasizes vertical push (60%) and vertical pull (80%).

The session position analysis reveals a consistent universal architecture: Mobility and Activation open the session; Power follows (performed fresh, never paired); Bilateral strength occupies the mid-session primary slot; Unilateral work, pull, push, and accessory work fill the late supplementary slots; and Core closes the session every time. Sprint, COD, and conditioning are typically programmed as separate sessions or at the beginning of field sessions before fatigue accumulates.

This frequency data provides the empirical foundation for the FORGE blueprint system: Core, Mobility, Bilateral Knee Dominant, Horizontal Pull, Activation, and Power are mandatory in every blueprint. Horizontal Push, Bilateral Hip Dominant, Unilateral Knee Dominant, and Sprint are strongly recommended with sport-specific consideration. Change of Direction, Vertical Pull, and Throw are optional additions based on sport context. Carry, Unilateral Hip Dominant, and Vertical Push are specialized movements for specific programming needs.
